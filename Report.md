# The Influenza Imperative

***An Urgent Need to Leverage Lessons from COVID-19 to Prepare for a Global Response to Seasonal and Pandemic Influenza***

## Introduction

The Influenza Imperative developed by the National Academy of Medicine. The paper builds on lessons from the COVID-19 pandemic to improve global preparedness for future influenza pandemics.
The objective of the manuscript was to synthesize four consensus studies and prioritize actionable recommendations aimed at strengthening pandemic preparedness, particularly in vaccine development, public health interventions, global coordination, and supply chain systems.

## Methodology

The paper employed a consensus-based approach rather than a traditional systematic methodology.

- An international committee convened a 3-day global workshop (2020).
- This led to the development of four consensus reports focusing on:
    - Vaccine research and development
    - Global coordination and financing
    - Vaccine manufacturing and supply chains
    - Non-vaccine public health interventions (e.g., masks, ventilation)

The output was a synthesis of 96 recommendations, later narrowed down to priority actions for immediate implementation.

## Recommentions.

1. Systemic Failures in Pandemic Preparedness
    • The COVID-19 pandemic exposed global unpreparedness, not just isolated weaknesses.
    • Surveillance systems, coordination mechanisms, and response infrastructure were insufficient.
2. Vaccine Inequity and Manufacturing Gaps
    • Low- and middle-income countries (LMICs), particularly in Africa, contributed ~1% of vaccine production.
    • Heavy reliance on imports delayed access and increased mortality risk.
3. Weak Supply Chains and Coordination
    • Global supply chains for vaccines and medical products were fragile and poorly coordinated.
    • Delays in distribution and shortages were widespread.
4. Underfunded Research and Development
    • Influenza and respiratory pathogen research remain underfunded, limiting preparedness for future pandemics.
5. Public Trust and Misinformation
    • Vaccine hesitancy was driven by misinformation and poor communication, affecting uptake.
6. Priority Recommendations Identified
    • Strengthening global surveillance systems
    • Establishing a global manufacturing and supply chain task force
    • Expanding vaccine production capacity
    • Improving public communication and trust-building

### Implications

**Policy and Governance**
    • Pandemic preparedness must be treated as a continuous global priority, not a reactive measure.
    • Governments should institutionalize preparedness through policy reform and governance structures.

**Health Systems Strengthening**
    • Need for integrated surveillance systems and real-time data sharing across countries.
    • Emphasis on a One Health approach linking human, animal, and environmental health.

**Equity and Access**
    • Policies must ensure equitable access to vaccines, therapeutics, and diagnostics across all countries.

**Financing**
    • Sustainable and long-term global financing mechanisms are essential to support preparedness efforts.

**Communication and Trust**
    • Strengthening community engagement and risk communication is critical to improving public trust and intervention uptake.

### Call to Action

The paper emphasizes urgent and coordinated global action, including:
    • Investing in surveillance systems for early detection of emerging pathogens
    • Expanding manufacturing capacity in LMICs through partnerships
    • Establishing resilient supply chains and stockpiles
    • Strengthening global coordination frameworks
    • Securing sustainable financing beyond donor dependence
    • Enhancing science communication to combat misinformation
The overarching message is to avoid repeating failures observed during COVID-19 and act proactively.

### Future of Research

Key research gaps identified include:

1. **Political Economy of Preparedness**
    • Why governments deprioritize pandemic preparedness between crises
    • Role of political and institutional incentives

2. **Vaccine Hesitancy and Behavioral Science**
    • Neurocognitive and sociocultural drivers of hesitancy
    • Spread of misinformation across digital platforms
    • Effectiveness of communication strategies in diverse contexts

3. **Vaccine Equity and Distribution Models**
    • Optimal allocation strategies during scarcity
    • Influence of geopolitical factors on access

4. **Manufacturing and Supply Chain Optimization**
    • Ideal geographic distribution of manufacturing hubs
    • Stress-testing supply chains during crises

5. **Digital Tools and Public Engagement**
    • Role of digital platforms in influencing public health responses

6. **Financing Models**
    • Development of sustainable and resilient funding frameworks for preparedness
